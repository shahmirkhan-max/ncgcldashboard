A Widespread Industrial Contraction

The period between July 2023 and June 2025 reveals a stark and troubling picture for Pakistan's industrial sector. This is not a cyclical downturn but a story of structural decay—a narrative of de-industrialization. Data from the Quantum Index of Manufacturing shows that a staggering 50% of the 86 industrial products analyzed are in a state of year-over-year (YoY) decline.

The overall industrial volume, as seen in the aggregate chart below, shows extreme volatility and a clear downward trajectory, signaling deep-seated instability. The cumulative effect of these widespread declines has resulted in virtually no growth in Large Scale Manufacturing (LSM) over the past two fiscal years.

This report contextualizes these figures, weaving a narrative that a confluence of crippling energy costs, macroeconomic instability, and incoherent trade policies is hollowing out the country's productive capacity. We will explore both broad sector-level indices and the performance of specific, hard-hit products to illustrate the depth of this crisis.

1. The Collapse of the Core: Steel and Chemicals

The health of a nation's industrial base can be measured by the strength of its foundational sectors. In Pakistan, this core is eroding at an alarming rate. The Steel & Metal sector index presents the most compelling evidence of this crisis. After a period of relative stability, the index goes into a freefall from February 2024. This is not a minor correction; it is a collapse. The chart for Billets/Ingots, a primary steel input, visually confirms this production crisis with a -21.9% YoY decline.

Similarly, the Chemicals & Intermediates sector, another cornerstone of industrial production, displays a sustained downward trend. The -22.6% YoY fall in Sulphuric Acid production underscores this weakness in a key industrial chemical.

3. Key Sectors in Distress: Textiles and Electronics

Even historically resilient sectors are showing signs of significant strain. The Textiles sector, long the backbone of Pakistan's exports, is now characterized by extreme volatility. While not in a state of freefall like steel, its erratic performance points to a loss of competitive advantage. The Electronics & Electrical sector is also in turmoil, reflecting its high dependency on imports, foreign exchange volatility, and the collapse in consumer purchasing power.

4. Even Seemingly Stable Sectors Show Weakness: The Case of Pharmaceuticals

On the surface, the overall Pharmaceuticals sector index appears to be one of the few areas bucking the trend of decline, showing periods of recovery. However, a deeper look into high-volume, essential products reveals a more concerning picture. Core items such as tablets, injections, and capsules have all seen significant YoY production declines. This suggests that while some niche or high-value products might be keeping the overall index afloat, the foundational elements of the pharmaceutical industry are under strain. This erosion in the production of basic medicines is another critical indicator of the country's weakening industrial capacity.

5. The Automotive Anomaly: A Sign of Distortion, Not Health

At first glance, the Vehicles & Auto sector appears to be a rare bright spot. However, this should not be mistaken for a sign of broad-based industrial health. This growth is more likely a product of market distortions, such as severe restrictions on the import of used vehicles, which artificially inflates demand for locally assembled cars. This outlier, therefore, serves to highlight policy inconsistencies rather than offering a counter-narrative to the broader story of decline.

Conclusion: An Economy at a Crossroads

The data in the QIM Chartbook does not depict a simple recession. It paints a clear picture of de-industrialization—a structural weakening of the country's ability to produce goods. The decline is broad and deep, affecting everything from foundational materials like steel and chemicals to everyday consumer products and essential medicines. With Large Scale Manufacturing showing no real growth for two years, the trend is undeniable.

This situation is the predictable outcome of a long-standing failure to address fundamental economic challenges. Without a serious and sustained policy shift focused on ensuring competitive energy tariffs, stabilizing the macro-economy, and creating a coherent long-term industrial strategy, this decline will continue. Pakistan is at a critical juncture, facing the prospect of losing its industrial base, which would have severe and lasting consequences for employment, exports, and overall economic sovereignty.
